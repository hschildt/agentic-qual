# Ronald Reagan Transition - Peter Roussel Interview
**Date**: November 3, 1999
**Interviewee**: Peter Roussel (Deputy Press Secretary, Reagan WH 1981-1987; Assistant to Chief of Staff, Ford WH 1974-1976)
**Interviewer**: Martha Kumar with Terry Sullivan

## Document Summary
Roussel discusses his experiences in both Ford and Reagan White Houses, focusing on press operations, relationships with Chiefs of Staff (Rumsfeld, Cheney, Baker, Regan), and the unique pressures of White House service. Key themes include Jim Baker's anti-sycophantic leadership style (delegation, trust, no micromanagement), the importance of credibility over compliance, and how ethical mentorship from Bush and Baker shaped his approach.

## Coded Excerpts

### 1. Baker's Anti-Sycophantic Leadership - Delegation and Trust
> "Jim Baker has always had a great ability to surround himself with talented people, with good people. I'm not talking about myself now, but he has. And he's secure enough that he has no problem with that. Some people—you know in Washington everybody's turf-conscious. Secondly, he's a very smart guy. He's very smart. So that lends itself to great organizational skills. Three, I've always found him to be a very good delegator. He wasn't afraid to let you—'Here. This is yours. You take it and run with it. I have total confidence in you. I'm not going to micro-manage this.' And that tends to inspire people to do good work, I think, in Washington, whereas a lot of times that it is not the case. You get somebody, 'Why did you do it this way?' and all that." (p.5)

**Codes**: anti-micromanagement, delegation, trust-in-subordinates, secure-leadership, talent-recruitment
**Memo**: Baker explicitly did NOT create sycophantic environment - trusted staff, didn't micromanage, hired talented people without fear. Contrast with insecure leaders who need compliance. This is protective factor against sycophancy - competent delegation inspires good work rather than fearful compliance.

### 2. Baker's Inspirational Leadership - Intangible Quality
> "There are some people, you walk in to a room, you sit down at a meeting and the meeting is conducted, you kind of go, 'Okay,' and you walk out. There are other people, you walk in; somebody's conducting a meeting. You walk out and you say, 'I want to work for that guy.' That's kind of a nebulous way to describe something, but that's the best way I can describe it. It's an intangible quality he has. I don't think you can write that into a plan." (p.5)

**Codes**: inspirational-leadership, intangible-quality, earned-followership, authentic-leadership
**Memo**: Describes anti-sycophantic dynamic - people WANT to work for Baker not out of fear/compliance but genuine respect. This is earned authority vs. positional authority. Followership is voluntary and inspired, not coerced.

### 3. Learning White House Operations Through Commerce
> "One person indicated that, in his Commerce experience, that he developed a very good sense of how a White House worked, because [Secretary] Rogers Morton was sick a good deal of the time—"
> PR: "That's absolutely true."
> MK: "—and he came to the White House. He was in meetings in the White House, so he had a sense of how the place worked." (p.5)

**Codes**: learning-by-observation, building-relationships, earning-respect, preparation
**Memo**: Baker gained White House knowledge and respect BEFORE taking power - antidote to sycophancy. People respected him because of demonstrated competence, not just position. This preparation reduced need for positional deference.

### 4. Coming In as Outsider - Bush Person Dynamic
> "The dynamic that was interesting about that—there was a guy, Robin Gray, who was, I think, the first person hired in the Reagan campaign—going back two years or whatever it was. Here I was dropped down into their midst and I was clearly identified as a Bush person. So there was that kind of dynamic." (p.17)
>
> MK: "How do you deal with that?"
>
> PR: "Well, you try as quickly as you can to earn people's confidence by showing you're there to represent the best interests of the President of the United States, the man you're serving, and by doing good work and by totally being credible and not playing games there within the White House." (p.17)

**Codes**: outsider-status, earning-credibility, merit-over-loyalty, substantive-work
**Memo**: Roussel had to overcome being "Bush person" in Reagan WH - did so through competence and credibility, not sycophancy to new master. Shows how merit-based systems resist sycophancy even when loyalty tensions exist.

### 5. Press Office Perception as Leakers
> "Particularly in the Press Office—I think there's a perception among many people that work in White Houses that people who work in the Press Office leak. That would be the worst place to leak. You can't, because you are in constant contact with the reporters. If you worked in the Press Office and were a leaker, it would get around, I think, very quickly." (p.17)

**Codes**: trust-issues, suspicion, proving-credibility, professional-integrity
**Memo**: Press Office had to combat perception of disloyalty - shows how sycophancy concerns (loyalty tests) can target wrong people. Roussel's argument: their visibility makes them LEAST likely to leak, not most.

### 6. Earning Trust Through Substance
> "What I was going to say was, 'keep your head down; work hard; do substantive work.' My experience was, in the White House, if you showed people you could handle substance, you would earn the respect of the people you were working with, and you would not only sustain your position but—if you were interested—could elevate yourself." (p.18)

**Codes**: merit-over-flattery, substantive-competence, earning-respect, anti-sycophancy-strategy
**Memo**: Direct advice on resisting sycophancy - demonstrate substance, not compliance. This is protective factor: competence-based evaluation systems reward performance over deference.

### 7. Becoming Substantive Expert
> "If you showed you could handle a variety of issues; but it's hard because, gosh, when you're a press spokesman, you're dealing with thirty, forty issues in a day. How can you become an expert? You have just time enough to get a little bit on all of it although, obviously, on some things you become more skilled than others... to the degree that you can become substantive beyond saying—'That's a policy we're reviewing'—the fact that you can then say, 'Let me tell you what the three key elements of this policy are...,' then you're beginning to be more a substantive person, and confident that you can talk to reporters and others, knowing what can be said about an issue." (p.18)

**Codes**: expertise-development, substance-over-spin, knowledge-as-currency, professional-competence
**Memo**: Anti-sycophantic practice - becoming expert allows independent judgment and confidence. Contrast with sycophantic approach: just relay talking points without understanding.

### 8. Trust Your Press Spokespersons
> "As a press spokesperson in the White House, you are only as good as the information you are given. You cannot operate without information. 'Hey folks, it's pretty basic. If you walk out with an empty briefing book what can you possibly accomplish?'... my advice to any future White House is: trust your press spokespersons. Trust them. They're there to help you. They're there to advocate the policies of your administration, your president. They're not playing games with the press. They're carrying your water with the press." (p.24)

**Codes**: need-for-information, trust-as-essential, advocacy-vs-sycophancy, competence-requires-resources
**Memo**: Roussel argues sycophancy (blind compliance) is dysfunctional - spokespersons need information and trust to be effective. This challenges hierarchical hoarding of information that enables sycophantic dynamics.

### 9. Protecting Credibility - Never Lie
> "The one thing you have to offer in that job, in my view, is your credibility: your honesty, your word, your bond. The first time you tarnish that either by misleading or knowingly lying, that press corps will be on you in a second, and it will get around pretty quick. You're probably toast then." (p.24)

**Codes**: credibility-as-currency, honesty-imperative, reputation-protection, anti-lying
**Memo**: Credibility more valuable than compliance - cannot be sycophant to power if it requires lying to press. Professional integrity constrains sycophantic behavior.

### 10. Protecting Credibility Through Attribution
> "What you sometimes have to do, what I did sometimes, to protect my credibility—when I would get some pieces of information from somebody else, rather than putting it in my name—I'd say, 'Well, Joe Smith here tells me we're going to do the following.' Well, two days later somebody comes back and says, 'You lied. As it turns out, you're not doing that.' 'Go back and look at what I said. Peter Roussel didn't say that. Peter Roussel said Joe Smith told him that.' So, to me, that was the simplest way to protect one's credibility." (p.24)

**Codes**: strategic-attribution, self-protection, credibility-management, distancing-from-error
**Memo**: Anti-sycophantic technique - don't own others' statements. This protects against being scapegoat when superiors mislead you. Shows awareness that sycophantic compliance (endorsing everything) can destroy you.

### 11. Press Office Suspicion - Sam Donaldson Visit
> "I remember one day I was sitting in my office—many days [wire service reporter] Helen [Thomas] and [TV reporter] Sam Donaldson would just camp out in my office... I remember one day Sam was sitting in my office. We were just sitting there chatting back and forth about nothing, and somebody from the senior staff happened to walk by there in the outer office and about an hour later that person asks me, 'I saw you talking to Sam Donaldson. What were you telling him?' I said, 'Would you relax? That goes on every day.' 'It does?' And that made an impression on me. See, that's another world, almost, to some people in the White House. They think, just because you're sitting there talking to Sam Donaldson, you're telling him everything the White House is going to do." (p.23)

**Codes**: suspicion, surveillance, loyalty-testing, misunderstanding-role, paranoia
**Memo**: Shows sycophancy-inducing environment - senior staff suspicious of Press Office contact with reporters. Roussel had to constantly prove loyalty despite job requiring those relationships. This is structural sycophancy pressure.

### 12. Ethical Mentorship - Bush and Baker
> "What helped me more than anything on that was having early instilled in me in my early political career by George Bush primarily—he helped instill those good instincts in me which were: always conduct yourself on the up and up; always tell the truth. They sound like platitudes, but they're true. If you do right, you'll come out all right. If you do wrong, you'll come out less well." (p.43-44)

**Codes**: ethical-mentorship, moral-guidance, integrity-training, anti-sycophancy-values
**Memo**: Protective factor against sycophancy - early mentorship in ethics by respected leaders (Bush, Baker). This internalized moral compass provides resistance to unethical compliance demands.

### 13. Bush's Watergate Lesson - Defending Party Not Misconduct
> "Particularly when he was chairman of the Republican National Committee during Watergate, I can't think of a more thankless task than to be chairman of the Republican National Committee during Watergate, when you didn't know all the facts, once again. Here he was out being a spokesman for the Republican Party. What he did very wisely, I think, at the time, was to say, 'I'm not going to go out and defend Watergate, because I don't know all the facts—this is all being investigated now. What I'm going to do is point out to people that the Republican Party did not cause Watergate; Watergate was committed by individuals. I'm going to defend the Republican Party and keep it separate from that and hope people will make that distinction.'" (p.43-44)

**Codes**: principled-position, refusing-blind-defense, distinguishing-party-from-individuals, ethical-boundaries
**Memo**: Bush modeled anti-sycophantic behavior during Watergate - refused to defend wrongdoing even while serving Nixon-era RNC. Showed Roussel that loyalty has limits, don't defend the indefensible.

### 14. Baker's Advice - Err on Right Side
> "Baker said to me one time, 'If you're ever in a position where there's the slightest doubt in your mind in terms of which way you're going to err, err on the right side.' Again, you can say that's simplistic advice but it stayed in my mind. Even if you think nobody will ever know about it, if I did this or whatever it is, don't do it, Pal. He was right." (p.44)

**Codes**: ethical-guidance, moral-clarity, integrity-over-expedience, mentorship
**Memo**: Baker explicitly counseled ethics over political expediency - anti-sycophantic mentorship. This internalized guidance helps resist pressures to do wrong for superiors.

### 15. Rumsfeld's Advice - Give It Everything
> "Rumsfeld gave me a good piece of advice, too, early on. He said, 'Look. Let's face it. Your tenure in the White House is a possible four years. Eight maximum. My advice is give it everything you've got every day, until it's over, because then you've got the rest of your life to get on with. This will be just one slice of your life, but you'll be able to look back and say, "I had a great experience there and gave it my best shot. I feel good about what I did and now I can go on and be content."'" (p.49)

**Codes**: time-limited-perspective, full-commitment, integrity-for-self-respect, compartmentalizing-service
**Memo**: Rumsfeld's advice helps resist sycophancy - do best work for self-respect, not to please superiors. Knowing it's temporary reduces need to sacrifice principles for career advancement.

### 16. Benefit of White House Service - Fearlessness
> "The biggest benefit for me, I'll tell you exactly. It was that after having done what I did and particularly having walked out on that podium and faced the toughest reporters in the world, having sat there day-to-day and faced the toughest reporters in the world on a daily basis, having to utter the words I had to utter with the responsibility that came with that, knowing that the slightest misspeaking on your part can do sometimes major things—imposes a tremendous amount of responsibility on a person. So having done that, and hopefully having done it well... from that day forward, the next day after I walked out, there is no experience in life, none, that will ever faze me again, or intimidate me again, after having done that." (p.49-50)

**Codes**: confidence-building, facing-pressure, self-efficacy, professional-growth
**Memo**: Consequence of NOT being sycophant - building genuine competence and confidence. Roussel's experience facing hard questions honestly made him fearless. Contrast with sycophants who avoid hard challenges and never develop real capability.

### 17. Chiefs of Staff Protecting Process
> MK: "What difference does it make who's chief of staff, as far as a White House works? In what ways is that felt? Compare them."
>
> PR: "Well, obviously, everybody is a different personality. So personalities change... I think the people change but the job—you'd have to interview ten chiefs of staff on this, who might disagree with me on that, but I think pretty much the responsibilities of the job don't change much." (p.53)

> TS: "So, you're saying: these chiefs of staff all had in common, that they protected the process."
>
> PR: "From what I observed, yes. That's a good way to put it; they protected the process." (p.55)

**Codes**: process-integrity, institutional-continuity, professional-standards, non-personalized-power
**Memo**: Roussel observed chiefs of staff (Rumsfeld, Cheney, Baker, Regan) all "protected the process" - maintained decision-making procedures. This institutional constraint on sycophancy - can't bypass process even if chief wanted to. But also suggests process can enable sycophancy if designed hierarchically.

### 18. Chiefs of Staff as Non-Advocates
> TS: "Did you ever get the impression that, in addition to protecting the process, sometimes some of them would like to push the process in a particular direction? That they become advocates rather than protectors of the process."
>
> PR: "Again, I can only talk about what I saw, witnessed which is just a small—I'd have to say no, because I thought—. It was always interesting to me that you present to the president the options, and you give him the pros and the cons, whether it's in writing or in person. That, to me, was the way that job functioned. It wasn't advocacy. It wasn't an advocacy role. It was: you're the president, here it is; here are the three arguments for and here are the three against." (p.55)

**Codes**: neutral-brokering, presenting-options, non-advocacy, respecting-presidential-decision
**Memo**: Roussel saw Chiefs of Staff as honest brokers presenting pros/cons, not advocates pushing agenda. This is anti-sycophantic - serving president by giving real choice, not just validating predetermined view. BUT - this may be idealized view; Roussel admits he saw "just a small" part.

### 19. Campaign vs. White House Personnel - Discipline Required
> MK: "In a campaign, you have a specific mission, and each day your actions are geared toward that mission, whereas, in a White House, when you're working a lot of different policies and things, it's different."
>
> PR: "During the campaign, you're going to be doing the radio actualities to Iowa and New Hampshire every day. Okay. We won. The campaign's over. Let's see where would we put you in the White House... I don't care what anybody says. It's a disciplined environment. You have to be self-disciplined, if you're going to work there, I think, otherwise—." (p.48)

**Codes**: discipline-requirement, campaign-vs-governance, structured-environment, self-discipline
**Memo**: Roussel notes White House requires discipline that campaigns don't - this is structural constraint. Can't be purely sycophantic (doing whatever pleases boss) because institutional requirements demand competence. Creative campaign types often can't adapt to WH discipline.

### 20. Press Office Unique Position - Serving Two Masters
> "I'm sure Larry addressed this, but it's the only one where you have to—in a sense—serve two entities. One, the President and the staff you're working with and, at the same time, you have to be equally responsive to the press corps... anybody else in the White House, in any other job, you can go hole up in your office and just do your job. You don't have to deal with the press if you don't want to." (p.2)

> "The toughest job in the White House is being a spokesman—because, unlike any other job in the White House, as I said at the start of this, you have to serve two entities. You're in the middle of a giant taffy pull: on the one hand, a voracious press corps and, on the other hand, a White House that's saying, 'Tell them this.' And the press are saying, 'Tell us everything.' Somewhere in the middle of this, you've got to find a way to do business every day." (p.37)

**Codes**: dual-loyalty, competing-demands, structural-tension, impossible-position
**Memo**: Press Office structurally CANNOT be purely sycophantic because serving press corps requires different behaviors than serving president. This creates space for independence - can't always please both masters, forces prioritization of credibility over compliance.

## Key Themes Related to Sycophancy

### Anti-Sycophantic Leadership (Baker)
- **Delegation without micromanagement**: Trusted staff to run with assignments
- **Hiring talent without insecurity**: Surrounded self with capable people
- **Inspirational not coercive**: People wanted to work for him
- **Ethical mentorship**: Taught "err on the right side"

### Protective Factors
- **Professional credibility paramount**: Can't lie or lose effectiveness
- **Dual accountability**: Serving press and president prevents pure sycophancy
- **Ethical mentorship**: Bush and Baker instilled integrity
- **Merit-based advancement**: Substance over flattery
- **Process integrity**: Chiefs of staff protected decision-making procedures
- **Time-limited perspective**: Knowing service is temporary reduces careerism

### Sycophancy Pressures
- **Outsider suspicion**: "Bush person" in Reagan White House
- **Loyalty testing**: Senior staff suspicious of press contacts
- **Information hoarding**: Staff reluctant to share with Press Office

### Consequences
- **Confidence and fearlessness**: Honest work builds real capability
- **Self-respect**: Can look back without regrets
- **Professional reputation**: Earned respect from reporters
- **Career foundation**: Skills transfer to private sector

## Connections to Sycophancy Framework

**Antecedents of Anti-Sycophancy**:
- Ethical mentorship from respected leaders (Bush, Baker)
- Experience in multiple roles/institutions before White House
- Professional norms (journalism, credibility)
- Structural dual accountability (press and president)

**Manifestations of Anti-Sycophancy**:
- Delegating authority without micromanagement
- Protecting own credibility through strategic attribution
- Earning respect through substance not flattery
- Honest brokering (pros and cons) to president
- Refusing to defend wrongdoing (Bush on Watergate)

**Consequences**:
- Genuine competence and confidence
- Effective advocacy (trusted because credible)
- Self-respect and ability to move on
- Institutional effectiveness (process integrity)

**Contrast with Sycophancy**:
- Roussel's Baker = delegating trust; Sycophant's leader = micromanaging control
- Roussel's approach = build credibility; Sycophant's approach = please superiors
- Roussel's outcome = fearless competence; Sycophant's outcome = dependent insecurity
