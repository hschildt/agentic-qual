# William J. Clinton Transition - Lloyd Cutler Interview
**Date**: July 8, 1999
**Interviewee**: Lloyd Cutler (White House Counsel, Carter 1979-1981 and Clinton 1994)
**Interviewer**: Martha Kumar with Nancy Kassop

## Document Summary
Cutler provides uniquely explicit discussion of sycophancy as endemic White House problem, plus extensive comparative analysis of Carter vs. Clinton counsel operations. Served in both administrations as White House Counsel, offering rare cross-administration perspective. Key themes: **explicit identification of sycophancy as "almost impossible to avoid,"** White House as disorganized "city hall," campaign people all thinking they're "top dog," obligations to "pay off people," counsel as potential "too good a soldier for his prince," experience as essential protective factor, corporate CEOs as poor temperamental fit, and erosion of executive privilege/institutional memory.

## Coded Excerpts

### 1. **EXPLICIT SYCOPHANCY STATEMENT** - "Very Sycophantic, Very Sycophantic"
> "There are really two or three qualities... you want somebody who has his own established reputation, especially now that presidents are put on the defensive so much about their personal past history and peccadilloes and whatever, and someone who is willing to stand up to the president, to say, 'No, Mr. President, you shouldn't do that, for these reasons.' **There is a great tendency among all presidential staffs to be very sycophantic, very sycophantic. It's almost impossible to avoid: 'This man is the President of the United States and you want to stay in his good graces....' Even when he's about to do something dumb; you don't tell him that. You find some way to put it in a very diplomatic manner.** So that's very important." (p.3)

**Codes**: explicit-sycophancy, impossible-to-avoid, stay-in-good-graces, presidential-deference, diplomatic-evasion, not-telling-truth, counsel-qualities
**Memo**: **CRITICAL EXCERPT** - Cutler explicitly names sycophancy as pervasive White House problem. "Very sycophantic, very sycophantic" = emphatic repetition. "Almost impossible to avoid" = structural inevitability, not individual failure. Mechanism: "This man is the President of the United States and you want to stay in his good graces." Fear of losing favor/access drives behavior. Manifestation: "Even when he's about to do something dumb; you don't tell him that. You find some way to put it in a very diplomatic manner." = euphemistic truth-telling, softening bad news rather than direct confrontation. Antecedent: presidential power + desire for good graces. Consequence: president doesn't get direct warnings. Protective factor: established reputation + willingness to stand up. This is single most explicit discussion of sycophancy in entire interview corpus.

### 2. White House as "City Hall" - Disorganization and Status Competition
> "The most important thing to grasp first is how much a White House itself, especially as it starts off after a change in the party occupying the White House, resembles a city hall. It is very, very difficult to organize. It isn't as if General Electric bought a company and sent in a management team that had worked together for twenty years... A new president naturally relies on the people who helped him get elected and also then seeks ethnic balance, geographic balance, leading public figures, experts in various fields. None of them know one another very well... **Everybody who comes in thinks he's chief of staff or deputy chief of staff and cannot bear to admit that he doesn't know what's going on, and will talk regularly to the press.** You have this tiny little West Wing with maybe fifteen, twenty offices in it... **Everybody who works for the President would rather be there than in the Executive Office Building... George Ball once put it very well when he said, 'Nothing propinques like propinquity'; everybody wants to be over on the other side.** People who are not in the West Wing come over there all the time for meetings, and they tend to hang around and visit people. They're standing in little groups and clumps all through the corridors... That's why I say it's like city hall in New York... So, by definition, it's extremely difficult to run." (p.1)

**Codes**: city-hall-chaos, organizational-difficulty, status-competition, proximity-power, cant-admit-ignorance, press-talking, propinquity, west-wing-status, disorganization
**Memo**: Vivid metaphor - WH = city hall, not corporate hierarchy. Multiple sycophancy dynamics: (1) **Status competition**: "Everybody thinks he's chief of staff" = inflated self-importance, inability to accept subordinate role. (2) **Cannot admit ignorance**: "cannot bear to admit that he doesn't know what's going on" = performative competence masking actual confusion. (3) **Press as status marker**: "will talk regularly to the press" = using media access to demonstrate importance (later elaborated p.11). (4) **Proximity = power**: Ball's "propinquity" quote - physical closeness to president = status symbol. West Wing > EEOB despite worse offices. Antecedents: campaign people don't know each other, no existing hierarchy, multiple power centers. Consequence: "extremely difficult to run." Contrast to GE analogy - no established team with 20-year working relationships. Sycophancy dimension: competing to appear important/knowledgeable rather than admitting limitations.

### 3. Campaign People "All Think They're the Top Dog"
> "MK: I guess people do tend to come from the campaign, but a lot of them know one another.
> LC: Some do; some don't. **They may have a passing acquaintance with one another, but they are not hierarchical among themselves. They all think they're the top dog.** The General Electric analogy is the best. That's what they're not." (p.2)

**Codes**: campaign-to-governance, non-hierarchical, top-dog-syndrome, status-equality, ge-analogy
**Memo**: Campaign people lack hierarchical ordering - "They all think they're the top dog." Nobody accepts subordinate status. "Not hierarchical among themselves" even if they know each other. Antecedent: campaign structure where people run parallel operations (NY team, CA team) without clear chain of command. Electoral victory creates sense of equal entitlement. Consequence: status competition, coordination problems. Contrast: GE management team = established hierarchy, clear roles, 20-year working relationships. Sycophancy dimension: everyone competing for presidential favor rather than accepting assigned roles.

### 4. Obligations to "Pay Off People"
> "There are some disadvantages, but mostly the advantage is: many of them are people the president knows, and has come to know intimately... It's somebody he's worked with, he feels comfortable with, whose judgment he relies on. That's why he picks those people. **But, also, he's incurred a lot of obligations during the campaign, and he has to pay off people. So even he will bring in people he doesn't know terribly well, or doesn't fully admire on the basis of experience.**" (p.2)

> "LC: Yes. It just goes with the territory. To govern, you've got to get elected. To get elected, you've got to have the campaign team, and **you have to incur certain promises and you have to reward the people that got you there.**" (p.2)

**Codes**: political-debt, pay-off-people, campaign-obligations, loyalty-rewards, not-fully-admire, promises-incurred
**Memo**: Explicit acknowledgment that loyalty trumps competence for some positions. "He has to pay off people" = transactional personnel decisions. "People he doesn't know terribly well, or doesn't fully admire on the basis of experience" = recognizing inadequacy but appointing anyway. "It just goes with the territory" = unavoidable political reality. "You have to reward the people that got you there" = loyalty as currency. Antecedent: campaign creates obligations/debts that must be honored. Consequence: positions filled by people president doesn't fully trust/admire. Sycophancy dimension: people appointed based on past loyalty rather than competence or candor. Creates staff who expect deference in return for campaign service.

### 5. "Too Good a Soldier for His Prince"
> "NK: Do you find that there's a difficulty, though, in reconciling the two; that sometimes advice that would be sound legal advice—
> LC: Sure. **And there are cases where you could argue that the White House Counsel is being too good a soldier for his prince. And there are other cases where the White House Counsel stopped something from happening that would have been a disaster.**" (p.4)

**Codes**: too-good-a-soldier, counsel-sycophancy, prince-metaphor, legal-political-tension, disaster-prevention
**Memo**: Explicit recognition that White House Counsel can be sycophantic - "too good a soldier for his prince." "Prince" = revealing monarchical metaphor. Tension between legal soundness and political loyalty. Two failure modes: (1) too compliant = "too good a soldier," enabling bad decisions; (2) too restrictive = preventing legitimate action. Protective factor: counsel who "stopped something from happening that would have been a disaster" = speaking truth even when unwelcome. Cutler acknowledges this as spectrum/trade-off rather than binary. Shows awareness that proximity and loyalty can corrupt legal judgment.

### 6. Press as Status Competition - "You Don't Want...to Think You're Out of Things"
> "What happens is that virtually everyone on the White House staff as a practical matter is available to the press and it takes you a long time to learn that there are those you can trust, and those you can't trust, that we're on background in different ways. **As I indicated earlier, especially in the early part of an administration, before it's shaken down, you don't want even the press to think you're out of things, that you don't know what's going on. So you tend to talk to anybody from the press who calls you and you say things that you've heard second- or third-hand, but that you don't really know.**" (p.11)

**Codes**: press-status-signaling, appearing-important, dont-know-but-talk, second-hand-info, early-admin-insecurity, shakedown-period
**Memo**: Press access as status marker creates incentive to talk even when uninformed. "You don't want even the press to think you're out of things, that you don't know what's going on" = appearance of importance > accuracy. Consequence: "you say things that you've heard second- or third-hand, but that you don't really know." Performative knowledge without substance. Antecedent: "early part of an administration, before it's shaken down" = insecure position, unclear pecking order. Self-protective behavior: demonstrate access/knowledge to press to signal internal status. Creates information problems (inaccurate leaks) and undermines discipline. Sycophancy dimension: performing importance rather than admitting limitations, similar to "cannot bear to admit that he doesn't know what's going on" (p.1).

### 7. "People Want to Be Important, Be Thought of as Important"
> "MK: Do you think that Democratic White Houses have more of a problem of that than Republican ones, that Republicans tend to run something of a tighter ship at the beginning?
> LC: It's hard to say. I don't think so, but **just as much in Republican administrations as in Democratic administrations, people want to be important, be thought of as important. Very often the leak is delivered to get something out in the open that you're against for policy reasons so you can knock it down.**" (p.11)

**Codes**: importance-seeking, bipartisan-phenomenon, leaking-for-status, trial-balloons, appearing-important
**Memo**: Status-seeking is bipartisan - "people want to be important, be thought of as important." Not Democratic-specific problem. Leaking as power move: "to get something out in the open that you're against for policy reasons so you can knock it down" = using press to fight internal battles. Sycophancy dimension: seeking external validation (press) for internal status. Undermines president's control of message. Shows how individual status-seeking conflicts with collective discipline.

### 8. Experience as Protective Factor - "By Making the Wrong Decisions"
> "You asked me earlier, and I didn't finish my answer: What are the qualities a counsel should have? **I think one of the qualities the Counsel would need is to have been in the White House before, not necessarily as counsel, but to have been through the mill before.**" (p.10)

> "MK: What does that provide you? What does previous experience provide you?
> LC: Well, I'll go through a famous Bernard Baruch story... Forrestal finally got to ask him a question which was, 'Mr. Baruch, how do you succeed on Wall Street?' And Baruch's answer was, 'By making the right decisions,' and he turned to somebody else. Forrestal got back in a bit later and said, 'How do you learn to make the right decisions?' Baruch's answer was, 'By experience,' and he turned away again. And finally Forrestal went back to ask the third question which was, 'How do you get experience?' **And of course the answer was, 'By making the wrong decisions.' That's what experience really means.**" (p.10)

**Codes**: experience-requirement, been-through-mill, learning-from-mistakes, baruch-story, protective-factor
**Memo**: Cutler argues counsel MUST have prior WH experience. "Been through the mill" = survived crises, made mistakes, learned. Baruch story: experience = making wrong decisions and learning. Anti-sycophancy mechanism: experienced people less likely to (1) be intimidated by presidential power, (2) make rookie mistakes from trying too hard to please, (3) need to prove themselves. Contrast to campaign people who may have political experience but not governing/WH experience. Experience provides: confidence to say no, understanding of consequences, established reputation that doesn't depend on current president's favor. Protective factor against sycophancy through security/confidence.

### 9. Executive Privilege and Short-Term vs. Long-Term Thinking
> "Having a sense of what the public reaction will be. A good example is executive privilege. For the sake of the office, it is very important that executive privilege be maintained and that the president can talk frankly, write memos, receive memos with his top advisers, without worrying that it will all have to be produced some day. But if he invokes it over and over again, the response from the public and the journalists and Congress is you're covering up; there must be something you have to hide. **The short-term costs of doing that have to be weighed against the long-term costs of protecting the confidentiality of discussions with the president. And it's a difficult choice to make. Usually, the lawyers say think of the long-term results; don't be in the position of waiving executive privilege. The president has to think of the short-term impact.**" (p.10)

**Codes**: executive-privilege, short-term-vs-long-term, public-reaction, legal-political-tension, institutional-protection
**Memo**: Tension between institutional interests (long-term) and political interests (short-term). Lawyers advocate for privilege to protect future presidents; president cares about immediate political cost. "Usually, the lawyers say think of the long-term results... The president has to think of the short-term impact." Sycophancy temptation: "too good a soldier" would prioritize president's short-term political needs over institutional long-term interests. Protective role: counsel who insists on protecting privilege even when politically costly. Similar to "protecting president's political capital" - sometimes means saying no.

### 10. Counsel as Both Policy and Politics - "Lawyers Are Not Bashful"
> "NK: So it would seem then that the White House Counsel's Office is really the mixture of politics and the legal aspects and the OLC is really just a legal—
> LC: That's right. **It's policy in the highest sense that has some legal aspect. Lawyers are not bashful about giving policy advice if they can find a legal basis to hang it on.**" (p.4)

**Codes**: policy-legal-mix, lawyers-not-bashful, legal-basis-for-policy, counsel-role, advocacy
**Memo**: Counsel role = hybrid legal/policy advisor. "Lawyers are not bashful about giving policy advice if they can find a legal basis to hang it on" = using legal authority to advocate policy preferences. Potential for "too good a soldier" - shaping legal advice to support president's desired policy. Contrast to OLC which is "just legal." Sycophancy risk: finding legal justification for what president wants rather than giving objective legal assessment. Requires integrity to maintain distinction.

### 11. Corporate CEOs as Poor Temperamental Fit
> "LC: Well, experience is one. **Lack of turf consciousness, lack of self-importance, ability to get on with a lot of diverse people, and knowing your President extremely well. Really, corporate chief executives are probably a bad stereotype for that job.**
> MK: Do you think that they just have too much of a sense of how they're going to run things?
> LC: **Yes. And they tend to lack government experience. Regan is a good example of that.** He came out of business. This is Donald Regan. He came out of business, went to the Treasury... Then he became White House chief of staff and **he tried to run it as if he were the admiral of the fleet. And various people like Baker and Deaver and Nancy Reagan just killed him.**" (p.25)

**Codes**: ceo-temperament, turf-consciousness, self-importance, regan-example, admiral-of-fleet, collaborative-requirement
**Memo**: Qualities needed: (1) **lack of turf consciousness** = not territorial, (2) **lack of self-importance** = ego control, (3) ability to get along with diverse people, (4) knowing president extremely well. CEOs fail because: (1) "too much of a sense of how they're going to run things" = command-and-control rather than collaborative, (2) lack government experience, (3) self-importance. Regan example: "tried to run it as if he were the admiral of the fleet" = military hierarchy inappropriate for political environment. Consequence: "Baker and Deaver and Nancy Reagan just killed him." Sycophancy dimension: self-important people expect deference rather than giving it. Also expect subordinates to be deferential, creating sycophantic culture. Collaborative environment requires mutual respect not hierarchy.

### 12. Lack of Institutional Memory - "It Certainly Could Be Improved"
> "LC: Well, some of them are historical, important on-going matters, and the files relating to those matters. While the president's library is entitled to those files, copies of a certain number of them are left from one administration to the other or from one counsel to the other. **But it's an inadequate system. As I've said earlier, the tendency to not write memos now is so strong. It certainly could be improved.**" (p.16)

> "**Presidents may have to respond to those subpoenas, certainly those that come from the criminal justice system. They have to respond. But it stops people from writing memos.** Many people came to me and said, 'Can they really look in my diary?' I said, 'I hope you don't keep a diary. Sure, they can look at your diary.' **Outside of the foreign policy field, the national security field, I would say many fewer memos are being written today, particularly relating to the various personal attacks on the president and investigations of the president. And that's too bad.**" (p.13)

**Codes**: institutional-memory-loss, fear-of-subpoenas, not-writing-memos, inadequate-system, documentation-decline
**Memo**: Vicious cycle: investigations → subpoenas → fear of documentation → no written record → institutional amnesia. "The tendency to not write memos now is so strong." "Many fewer memos are being written today... And that's too bad." Consequence: each administration reinvents wheel, no learning from past mistakes, harder for counsel to give informed advice. Cutler earlier noted he created "brain book" himself - should have been provided. Sycophancy connection: oral culture makes it easier to tell president what he wants to hear without record. Written advice = accountability. Fear of subpoenas creates incentive to avoid creating evidence of disagreement/pushback.

### 13. Longer Transition Needed - "Looks as if You Have a Lot of Hubris"
> "LC: Well, a longer transition period would be a good idea. If presidents started a trusted group transitioning before the election, that would be a good idea, **but it looks as if you have a lot of hubris if you do that, you think you're going to win the election.**
> MK: [Ronald] Reagan was able to do it. He put a team together starting in 1979. It worked to his advantage; there didn't seem to be any disadvantages from it. He didn't get the newspapers articles—
> LC: That's true." (p.2)

**Codes**: transition-length, pre-election-planning, hubris-appearance, reagan-exception, sixty-day-problem
**Memo**: 60-80 days inadequate to build functioning team. Pre-election planning = good governance but "looks as if you have a lot of hubris." Reagan did it successfully without criticism. Appearance concern prevents good preparation. Consequence: rushed personnel decisions, inadequate vetting, disorganized start. Sycophancy connection: short transition favors campaign loyalists (already known) over competent outsiders (need time to recruit/vet). Insufficient time to build structure that could check sycophancy.

### 14. Clinton vs. Carter - Defense vs. Substance
> "NK: Could you give a comparative percentage of the time on substantive—
> LC: **I would say working for Carter - which was a year and a half - not more than 20 per cent was what I call playing defense. Under Clinton it was closer to 80 per cent.**" (p.6)

**Codes**: carter-vs-clinton, defense-vs-substance, investigations-burden, time-allocation, whitewater-impact
**Memo**: Dramatic shift in counsel role between administrations. Carter: 20% defense (Billy Carter, Hamilton Jordan allegations), 80% substantive policy. Clinton: 80% defense (Whitewater, impeachment), 20% substantive. "Under Clinton it was closer to 80 per cent" playing defense = investigations consuming counsel office. Consequence: no time for proactive legal-policy work, counsel becomes reactive crisis manager. Cutler: "I had to put in so much of my own daily effort and my staff did, on the investigations of the President, Whitewater, et cetera, that I had no time." Sycophancy dimension: defensive posture may make counsel more protective of president, less willing to challenge. Also: partisan warfare creates siege mentality.

### 15. Impeachment as Partisan Weapon - Weakening Presidency
> "Well, I think we have witnessed a serious abuse of the impeachment process by the Republican majority, or the radical silent Republican majority **for the intended purpose of weakening the president. If you didn't drive him from office at least you weakened his ability to carry out his programs.** And that's a tendency we're going to see more and more... **Impeachment has now—because of this, the first impeachment in 100 years, it's now become a weapon with which to attack the incumbent president of the other party.**" (p.14)

**Codes**: impeachment-as-weapon, partisan-warfare, weakening-president, republican-strategy, institutional-damage
**Memo**: Cutler sees Clinton impeachment as partisan strategy to weaken president, not principled constitutional process. "Intended purpose of weakening the president." "Now become a weapon with which to attack the incumbent president of the other party." Predicts this will continue: "Democrats will do it to some future Republican president." Institutional degradation: impeachment (rare, serious) → partisan tool (routine, political). Sycophancy dimension: partisan warfare creates siege mentality, "us vs. them" thinking. Staff loyalty to president becomes loyalty against enemies. Defensive crouch may intensify sycophantic protectiveness rather than objective judgment.

### 16. Physical and Psychological Toll
> "The hours are really much longer. You're there for a 7:30 a.m. staff meeting, which means you have to get up pretty early. You're there at 7:00 or 8:00 o'clock at night. People like Stu Eizenstat were there until 10:00, 11:00, 12:00 almost every night... **But most of all it is the fact that you are constantly on call and you're constantly making snap judgments. You're fencing with the press. You're acting on the basis of not enough information and there's always this gnawing fear that you've gotten something wrong or you've said something you shouldn't have said.**" (p.23)

**Codes**: physical-toll, long-hours, snap-judgments, constant-vulnerability, fear-of-mistakes, press-fencing
**Memo**: Extreme stress: early mornings (7:30am meetings), late nights (7-8pm minimum, some until midnight), constant availability, insufficient information, fear of errors. "Gnawing fear that you've gotten something wrong or you've said something you shouldn't have said." Psychological pressure of high-stakes decisions on incomplete information. "It's all day, in a sense, that you're vulnerable." Sycophancy connection: exhaustion + fear → risk aversion → tendency to go along rather than fight. Physical/mental depletion reduces capacity for independent judgment. Fear of mistakes may lead to seeking presidential approval for safety.

### 17. Two-Year Limit on White House Service
> "MK: Some people have been recommending that a White House job is about a two-year stint, and that it's hard to go beyond that.
> LC: **It ought to be more but it is certainly true that even people who are very, very good at it and very, very relaxed temperaments, like a Bob Rubin or Jim Baker, were glad to get out of the White House and take on a major Cabinet position instead.**" (p.24)

**Codes**: two-year-limit, burnout, cabinet-relief, rubin-baker-examples, wh-intensity
**Memo**: Even highly competent, "very relaxed temperaments" eager to leave WH for Cabinet. Cabinet advantages: "your own security, your own car, these beautiful offices, the fact that you control your agenda" vs. WH "little cubbyholes... so touch-and-go." Suggests WH intensity unsustainable long-term. Sycophancy implication: turnover means constant influx of inexperienced people vulnerable to sycophancy (per earlier point about experience as protective factor). Institutional memory loss. Short tenure may also make people more risk-averse (want to survive their stint without mistakes).

### 18. Presidential Temperament - Clinton's Volcanic Temper (Reported, Not Witnessed)
> "MK: ...one of the things that I wonder about is, what is that like? What kind of environment is that like, when you have to deal with a person who has such an enormous temper?
> LC: **Well, in my six months I never saw him lose his temper, either with me or anyone else he was dealing with. Every now or then, he'd get going on the Republican right or his enemies in Arkansas or something like that. But I certainly heard a lot of stories about his temper; I just never witnessed it personally.**" (p.29)

**Codes**: clinton-temper, volcanic-temperament, personal-experience, heard-stories, managing-president
**Memo**: Cutler never personally witnessed Clinton temper despite "a lot of stories about his temper." Caveat: only there 6 months, may not be representative. Kumar's question references others saying you have to let Clinton "explode" in private, "sand down a response" before public statement. Sycophancy dimension: if president has volatile temper, creates incentive to avoid confrontation, tell him what he wants to hear. "Walk through a question; he has to be allowed to explode" = managing presidential emotions rather than giving direct advice. Cutler's non-experience may reflect: (1) short tenure, (2) established reputation protecting him, (3) "Clark Clifford role" gave him special status.

### 19. "Clifford Principle" - Using Historical Precedent for Access
> "LC: Some. When I was asked by the President to take this job, it was a mid-life crisis of his administration, the so-called 'malaise' period. I said, 'What kind of a role do you want me to play?' I knew him, but I didn't know him that well. He said, 'I want you to play sort of a Clark Clifford role.' **I got that in writing and, of course, Clifford was so venerable and such a great storyteller, everybody thought that Harry Truman never made a move without consulting Clark Clifford. And every time I got left out of a meeting I would go to Jordan or I would go to the President, and I would say, 'I think that Harry Truman would have wanted Clark Clifford in this meeting.' I was older than all the rest of them, so nobody could gainsay me.**" (p.8)

**Codes**: clifford-principle, historical-precedent, access-protection, in-writing, age-authority, meeting-access
**Memo**: Brilliant anti-sycophancy tactic: establishing role expectation in writing, then using historical precedent to maintain access. "I got that in writing" = documentation of presidential commitment. "Every time I got left out of a meeting I would go... and say, 'I think that Harry Truman would have wanted Clark Clifford in this meeting.'" Using Clifford mythos (venerable, trusted advisor) to claim meeting access. "I was older than all the rest of them, so nobody could gainsay me" = age/experience as authority. Protective factor: pre-negotiated role + willingness to assert it. Most staff wouldn't challenge being excluded. Shows importance of establishing expectations upfront and defending them.

### 20. Success = "I Survived"
> "MK: How would you judge success in your operation? What constitutes success?
> LC: **A minimum level is the Abbé Seyez— his comment about what did you do in the French Revolution, 'I survived.' I suppose success is getting out of it alive, without any damage to your reputation and without any damage to the president's reputation, and to feel you played a part in the making of policy. You're always on the frontier of events.**" (p.27)

**Codes**: survival-as-success, reputation-protection, frontier-of-events, low-bar, defensive-metric
**Memo**: "Minimum level... 'I survived.'" Modest definition of success: (1) survival, (2) reputation intact (personal), (3) reputation intact (presidential), (4) played policy role. Note: not "achieved policy goals" or "improved governance" but simply surviving without damage. Reflects defensive, risk-averse environment. "Always on the frontier of events" = constant high-stakes decisions. Sycophancy dimension: survival-focused culture creates risk aversion, tendency to avoid conflict/confrontation. Better to go along and survive than challenge and fail.

### 21. No Institutional Reflection - "There Ought to Be, But There Isn't"
> "MK: Is there a process in either White House that you worked in for looking backwards, for looking at how well things worked, what could have been done better?
> LC: **No. There ought to be, but there isn't.** And it's better done, I think, by outside people than anyone doing it officially for the President, especially these days, when papers get subpoenaed... Today, some Congressional committee would quickly get hold of it." (p.27)

**Codes**: no-reflection, ought-to-be, outside-evaluation, subpoena-fear, no-learning
**Memo**: Zero institutional learning processes. "There ought to be, but there isn't." Carter State Dept tried to write "white paper" on Iran relationships but never published - "events were ongoing and people thought publishing it would do some damage." Fear: "Today, some Congressional committee would quickly get hold of it." Consequence: same mistakes repeated, no organizational learning. Sycophancy connection: without formal evaluation, informal presidential favor becomes only feedback mechanism. No objective assessment of what worked/didn't work. Staff judged by whether president pleased, not whether outcomes achieved. Reinforces sycophantic incentives.

## Summary Analysis

This is the **single most explicit and comprehensive discussion of sycophancy** in the entire interview corpus. Cutler's unique dual-administration perspective (Carter and Clinton counsel) provides rare comparative insight.

### Key Sycophancy Points:

1. **Explicit Naming**: "Very sycophantic, very sycophantic. It's almost impossible to avoid." Only interviewee to directly name sycophancy as pervasive problem.

2. **Mechanism**: "This man is the President of the United States and you want to stay in his good graces." Power differential + desire for favor = sycophancy.

3. **Manifestation**: "Even when he's about to do something dumb; you don't tell him that. You find some way to put it in a very diplomatic manner." Euphemistic truth-telling.

4. **Structural Causes**:
   - White House as "city hall" - disorganized, status-competitive
   - Campaign people "all think they're the top dog" - non-hierarchical
   - Obligations to "pay off people" - loyalty over competence
   - Press as status marker - performative importance
   - "Cannot bear to admit...don't know what's going on"

5. **Protective Factors**:
   - Experience ("been through the mill")
   - Established reputation
   - Willingness to stand up to president
   - Lack of turf consciousness/self-importance
   - Knowing president "extremely well"
   - Getting role in writing ("Clifford Principle")

6. **Failure Modes**:
   - "Too good a soldier for his prince"
   - CEOs expecting deference rather than collaboration
   - Short-term political thinking over long-term institutional interests

7. **Environmental Factors**:
   - Physical/psychological toll → exhaustion → risk aversion
   - Fear of subpoenas → no written record → less accountability
